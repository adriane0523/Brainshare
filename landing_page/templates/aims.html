Project Aims

Project Narrative Project Aims Modified Policy Delphi Importance

Data sharing is essential to promote equity and maximize the impact of the significant investment in the BRAIN Initiative. Data sharing plans are now required for BRAIN funding, but there is an urgent need to develop specific policies and practices that are empirically informed and address ethical challenges and concerns related to sharing human data generated from BRAIN Initiative research. In its 2019 Neuroethics Roadmap, the Advisory Committee to the Director of NIH BRAIN 2.0 Neuroethics Subgroup (BNS) identified developing such standards for the “neuroscience data ecosystem” as one of five key priorities for the BRAIN Initiative. The BNS specifically calls out the “ethical imperative” to share invasive neural recordings given their rarity, while acknowledging concerns about sharing these types of data because they may “gain insight into an individual’s thoughts.” Data sharing has been the object of study in other fields, such as genomics, but there are distinctive features of research on innovative neurotechnologies in the BRAIN Initiative that are likely to present unique challenges and raise new concerns. Our own experience and research with investigators conducting BRAIN Initiative-funded studies of closed loop or adaptive deep brain stimulation suggests that the practice of sharing data is inconsistent and incomplete, despite broad agreement that it is important. Some ethical challenges raised include issues related to privacy, consent, interoperability, and competing commercial and professional interests. As the BNS points out, ensuring equity and maximizing benefits from the large public investment in the BRAIN Initiative depends on better understanding such challenges, then formulating policies and best practices that address them.

The long-term goal of our research program is to develop, test, and disseminate strategies to improve data sharing in biomedical research. Our previous work (R01HG004333, R01HG006460; R01HG08918; R01CA237118) addresses ethical and policy challenges to sharing genomic data to advance precision medicine. The objective of this proposal is to apply our combined expertise in neuroscience, neuroethics, social science, law, and science policy, and our extensive experience studying data sharing, to identify challenges and concerns and generate empirically-informed policy and practice options that facilitate responsible data sharing within the BRAIN Initiative.

Aim 1: Examine current policy and practice and identify challenges to sharing human data within the BRAIN Initiative. We will conduct stakeholder interviews and analyze relevant documents governing data sharing (e.g., consent forms, data sharing policies, industry contracts) within the BRAIN Initiative. Drawing on our previous work, we will identify challenges, as well as policy and practice considerations specific to BRAIN Initiative research.

Aim 2: Assess BRAIN Initiative research participants’ attitudes, preferences, and concerns about data sharing and brain privacy. We will conduct qualitative interviews (n=20) and survey (n=100) a diverse cohort of BRAIN Initiative research participants to investigate their attitudes, preferences, and prioritization of concerns, as determined from stated preference methods. Aim 3: Generate empirically-informed policy and practice options that address high-priority challenges to sharing human data from BRAIN Initiative research. We will employ a modified policy Delphi process with diverse stakeholders: BRAIN Initiative-funded investigators, patient advocates, industry representatives, data stewards, and neuroethics and law/policy scholars. In successive rounds, panelists will prioritize challenges to sharing human BRAIN data; evaluate how well existing policies and practices address high- priority challenges; and generate policy and practice options to address identified gaps and inconsistencies.

This project is urgent and significant because the stakes are very high: adoption of policies and practices that do not reflect understanding of stakeholder perspectives can compromise trust and compound inequities. Once the data sharing infrastructure is fully built out, change will be much more difficult. Hence, this is a critical time for proactively engaging stakeholders in anticipating and resolving challenges. By tailoring our work to specific data types and uses of ongoing relevance for the BRAIN Initiative, this project will contribute to maximizing the value of BRAIN Initiative-generated data. This project is innovative in its engagement with multiple diverse stakeholder groups and its use of mixed methods incorporated into a modified policy Delphi framework. The work is feasible in our hands as demonstrated by the productivity of this team in prior work, as well as our collective data sharing and neuroethics expertise, and experience with empirical social science methods.
